§01

Structure

Jo Ann Beard — "The Fourth State of Matter"(1996, The New Yorker)

Tense Present tense throughout, even for the central tragedy Narrator 1st person

Braid several unrelated threads together to delay the real subject as long as possible.

  1. Open on the most minor thread (an aging dog waking her at night) with no hint of what's coming.
  2. Introduce two more threads in parallel — squirrels living in the house, a collapsing marriage — cutting between them like a film edit.
  3. Let a fourth, much heavier thread (a colleague, a workplace, a shooter) enter as just one more strand, given no more narrative weight than the others at first.
  4. Slip briefly into another person's consciousness (the shooter's) without warning or transition, then return to her own present tense.
  5. Let the threads converge only near the end, so the reader feels the collision the way the narrator did — unprepared.

Test it — Braid two unrelated ongoing situations in your life with one much heavier one, giving all three equal weight at first.

Tim O'Brien — "How to Tell a True War Story"(1990, The Things They Carried)

Tense Past tense stories, present tense commentary Narrator 1st person, self-consciously unreliable — fiction

Interrupt the story to argue about how true stories should be told, then contradict your own rules.

  1. Open by flatly asserting the story is true, before any story has been told.
  2. Tell a version of the story, then step back into commentary about what makes war stories true or false.
  3. Retell the same death from a different angle, adding or changing details each time.
  4. State a rule about storytelling (a true war story has no moral), then later break that exact rule.
  5. End by declaring the story invented, beginning to end — reversing the story's own opening claim.

Test it — Tell a personal story, stop to state a rule about how such stories should be told, then break your own rule before you finish.

Kate Chopin — "The Story of an Hour"(1894)

Tense Past tense Narrator 3rd person limited — fiction

Compress an entire emotional arc — grief, secret joy, collapse — into roughly a thousand words and a single hour.

  1. Open with the news itself, delivered as gently and quickly as the plot requires — no lingering on the messenger.
  2. Move the character alone into a private space where her real reaction can happen unobserved.
  3. Let a physical, external detail (a window, spring air) carry the emotional turn instead of stating the emotion directly.
  4. Name the forbidden feeling only once, in the character's own private repeated word, and let repetition do the intensifying.
  5. Reverse the situation in one sentence at the very end, and let the final line's irony do all the closing work.

Test it — Compress a full emotional turn — grief, relief, or shock — into a single scene under 1,000 words, staged in four movements.

§02

Tone vs. Mood

Shirley Jackson — "The Lottery"(1948, The New Yorker)

Tense Past tense Narrator 3rd person, communal — fiction

Keep the narrating tone bright and pleasant for the entire piece while the mood underneath builds from unease to horror.

  1. Open with cheerful, ordinary scene-setting — good weather, neighbors gathering — and never let the tone drop this register.
  2. Insert a small strange detail early (children collecting stones) without comment, so it reads as innocent on first pass.
  3. Keep dialogue folksy and joking even as the ritual's actual stakes become clear to the reader.
  4. Let procedural, bureaucratic detail (an old box, a checklist of names) carry the dread instead of any narrated fear.
  5. Hold the tone steady all the way through the final action — do not let the narrating voice flinch even at the end.

Test it — Narrate a disturbing event in the same cheerful, matter-of-fact tone you'd use for something pleasant. Never let the tone crack.

George Orwell — "Shooting an Elephant"(1936)

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person, self-critical

Keep the narrating tone analytical and self-diagnosing while the mood stays oppressive and absurd.

  1. Open by naming your own resentment and complicity plainly, before any incident occurs.
  2. Narrate the inciting event (an elephant on a rampage) with procedural calm, even as the situation escalates.
  3. State your private reasoning (I knew I shouldn't shoot him) clearly, then describe doing the opposite because of the crowd.
  4. Diagnose your own motive in cool, essayistic language, even while the action itself remains tense and physical.
  5. Refuse a redemptive ending — end on the same self-aware, faintly bitter tone you began with.

Test it — Narrate a moment you acted against your own judgment because of social pressure, staying analytical and self-critical throughout — no self-forgiveness.

E.B. White — "Once More to the Lake"(1941, Harper's)

Tense Past tense frame, present-tense sensory immersion Narrator 1st person

Keep the tone warm and nostalgic while the mood quietly shifts toward mortal dread.

  1. Open with long, comma-heavy, unhurried sentences describing a beloved unchanged place.
  2. Blur the narrator's identity with his son's, letting pronouns and generations slide into each other without alarm.
  3. Note small changes (a new engine sound) in the same warm register used for everything else — don't flag them as ominous.
  4. Let the nostalgic tone continue uninterrupted even as the underlying subject (mortality) becomes clear to the reader.
  5. Land the mood shift in a single short, unadorned final sentence, with no tonal warm-up beforehand.

Test it — Write a warm, nostalgic scene revisiting somewhere from childhood, and land on one short unadorned sentence that reveals the dread underneath.

Leslie Jamison — "The Empathy Exams"(2014, The Believer)

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person

Interleave bureaucratic training-script tone with raw personal narrative to make tone and mood pull apart on purpose.

  1. Introduce a formal, institutional document (a training script, a checklist) as its own labeled section.
  2. Write that inserted section in flat, procedural, second-person instructional tone.
  3. Cut to first-person narrative describing a real, emotionally loaded personal event, in a completely different register.
  4. Alternate between the two tones several times without transitions, letting the reader feel the whiplash.
  5. Let the essay's real argument surface in the gap between the clinical tone and the raw mood, never stated outright.

Test it — Write two alternating sections on the same subject — one in flat institutional/instructional language, one in raw first-person feeling — and let them sit side by side unresolved.

§03

Persona-Narrator & Voice

Jamaica Kincaid — "Girl"(1978, The New Yorker)

Tense Present tense, imperative Narrator 2nd person — one continuous maternal voice, fiction

Write the entire piece as one unbroken sentence of second-person instruction.

  1. Open mid-instruction, with no framing or introduction of who is speaking to whom.
  2. Alternate practical domestic instructions with moral warnings, in the same flat instructive register.
  3. Let a single line of pushback from the addressee interrupt once, then let the instructing voice absorb and continue past it.
  4. Vary instruction length — some clauses very short, some much longer — to keep the single sentence from flattening into monotony.
  5. End without resolution or softening; the instructing voice does not pause to comfort or explain itself.

Test it — Write a full page as one continuous sentence of second-person instructions from a specific person in your life, without a single period until the end.

Sherman Alexie — "Superman and Me"(1998, Los Angeles Times)

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person, defiant

Build the whole essay on one literal extended metaphor that starts small and grows to justify the essay's shape.

  1. Introduce the controlling image (a paragraph) as a private, almost childish way of understanding one small thing.
  2. Apply the same image to something larger (a family) to show the metaphor can scale.
  3. Let the image justify a leap in subject (from family to comic book to literacy itself) without announcing the leap.
  4. Use short, repeated declarative sentences (I was smart. I was arrogant. I was lucky.) as a rhythmic anchor.
  5. Repeat that same declarative triad later with one word changed, so the reader feels the essay's argument evolve without being told it evolved.

Test it — Pick one small, literal image from childhood and use it to structure an essay about something much larger, letting the image scale up without announcing the jump.
