The Four Foundational Patterns

Joan Didion — "Goodbye to All That / The White Album"

Tense Older narrator in present tense, framing scenes told in past tense Narrator 1st person, older-younger — the narrator who is thinking now about then

Open with confident authority — a claim about what "we" all believe or do. Let that authority quietly leak away until a singular, less certain "I" is what's left standing.

  1. Opens on a general, authoritative claim ("We tell ourselves...")
  2. A small hinge phrase lets the certainty slip ("Or at least we do for a while...")
  3. Singular "I" enters, marking a specific time of doubt
  4. Facts and events are listed in flat, matter-of-fact language — the emotion is never named, only assembled
  5. Present-tense reminders ("It was a time of my life...") keep signaling that this is the older self narrating
  6. Ends on dry understatement that undercuts or reframes everything before it

Test it — Your material is about a belief, identity, or self-image you've quietly stopped believing in.

James Baldwin — "Notes of a Native Son"

Tense Past tense throughout, but carrying an older narrator's hindsight Narrator 1st person — intelligent, passionate, looking back at a defining relationship

Drop the reader straight into a dated, specific scene with no warm-up. Let short factual sentences accumulate into longer, more emotionally loaded ones as context builds.

  1. Opens in medias res, often with an exact date and a stark fact
  2. Short declarative sentences establish the concrete situation
  3. Historical/generational context widens the frame (what was true for the parent that isn't true for the narrator)
  4. Sentence length and clause complexity grow as the emotional stakes rise
  5. The narrator's present-day understanding breaks through the past-tense surface, but rarely as direct commentary — it's embedded in word choice
  6. Closes without full resolution — relief, grief, and unfinished feeling can coexist

Test it — Your material is a relationship or event you're still making sense of, especially across a generational or historical divide.

Eula Biss — "Time and Distance Overcome"

Tense Past tense for historical fact, present tense when the personal narrator briefly appears Narrator Largely absent — an unnamed collector of facts until late, when a 1st person "I" and "my sister" arrive

Withhold the personal "I" almost entirely. Let short, flat, factual paragraphs stack against each other until the pattern itself becomes unbearable. Only then let the narrator step in.

  1. Opens with an ordinary, even dry historical fact (an invention, a date)
  2. Facts accumulate in short paragraphs — no transitions, no commentary, just juxtaposition
  3. A dark pattern emerges purely through repetition and placement, never through the narrator saying "notice this"
  4. The turn is a single plain sentence bringing in personal/family material ("My grandfather was a lineman.")
  5. Brief present-tense personal reflection, restrained, almost as flat as the facts before it
  6. Ends on an image that doesn't explain itself — no moral, no summary

Test it — Your subject is too large, political, or painful to approach directly — when the facts need to do the emotional work before the "I" is allowed to speak.

Annie Dillard — "Total Eclipse"

Tense Past tense, narrated from two years later, with liberties taken with chronological order Narrator 1st person, past tense, retrospective — a narrator very aware she is choosing what to withhold

Refuse to explain. Open with a strange, vivid, seemingly unrelated image (a vegetable clown painting) and only gradually loop back to explain how you got there — withholding the real subject as long as possible.

  1. Opens with a simile comparing the experience to dying or falling — before naming what "the experience" even was
  2. Places the reader in a specific, disorienting scene (a hotel room, an odd painting) with no explanation of relevance
  3. Explicitly signals retrospective narration ("Two years have passed since...")
  4. Backtracks non-chronologically to the danger/journey that preceded the scene just shown
  5. Formal, slightly elevated diction mixed with dry humor and internal rhyme
  6. Sections build dream-like disorientation before finally arriving, late, at the event itself

Test it — You want the reader to feel disoriented and unprepared the way you were — when the "point" of the piece should arrive late, after atmosphere and delay have done their work.
