§01

Structure & Opening Strategies

Joan Didion — "Goodbye to All That"

Tense Older narrator, present tense, framing past-tense scenes Narrator 1st person, older-younger

Confident general authority erodes into a singular, doubting "I."

  1. Open on a sweeping, confident claim — something "everyone" believes or does, stated flatly.
  2. Drop in a small hinge phrase that quietly loosens the certainty ("Or at least we do for a while...").
  3. Introduce the singular "I" at the exact moment doubt enters — this is the pivot of the whole piece.
  4. List concrete facts and daily routines in flat, unemotional language; let the emotion live in the gap between the facts and how calm the voice sounds.
  5. Close on dry understatement that reframes everything before it, without explaining the reframe.

Test it — Write one confident opening sentence about something you used to believe, then write the exact sentence where the belief cracked.

James Baldwin — "Notes of a Native Son"

Tense Past tense, carrying an older narrator's hindsight Narrator 1st person, looking back at a defining relationship

Drop the reader into a dated scene with no warm-up; let sentence length grow as stakes rise.

  1. Open with an exact date and a stark, undeniable fact — no throat-clearing, no context first.
  2. Follow with short declarative sentences that establish the concrete situation.
  3. Widen the frame with historical or generational context (what was true for the parent, not the narrator).
  4. Let sentences grow longer and more clause-heavy as the emotional stakes increase — syntax should mirror feeling.
  5. End without full resolution; let contradictory emotions (relief and grief) sit side by side.

Test it — Open with a specific date and one hard fact about that day. Write five short sentences, then let the sixth run long.

Eula Biss — "Time and Distance Overcome"

Tense Past tense for historical fact; present tense when the "I" briefly appears Narrator Largely absent — an unnamed collector of facts until late

Withhold the "I" almost entirely; let accumulated facts do the emotional work.

  1. Open on a dry, ordinary historical fact — no personal voice at all.
  2. Stack more facts in short paragraphs with zero transitions or commentary between them.
  3. Let a dark pattern emerge purely from placement and repetition — never state the pattern outright.
  4. Introduce the "I" late, in one plain declarative sentence that connects the facts to a real person ("My grandfather was a lineman.")
  5. End on a small, unexplained image that doesn't resolve the tension — no moral, no summary.

Test it — List five factual sentences on a topic with zero commentary, then land one flat personal sentence that connects them to you.

Annie Dillard — "Total Eclipse"

Tense Past tense, narrated from two years later; chronology deliberately scrambled Narrator 1st person, retrospective, withholding on purpose

Open with a strange, vivid, unrelated image and delay the real subject as long as possible.

  1. Open with a simile comparing the experience to something ordinary (dying, falling) before naming what happened.
  2. Place the reader inside a disorienting scene (a hotel room, a strange painting) with zero explanation of relevance.
  3. Explicitly signal that this is retrospective narration ("Two years have passed since...").
  4. Backtrack non-chronologically to the events that preceded the scene already shown.
  5. Let formal diction and dry humor coexist; build dream-like disorientation before finally arriving at the real event, late.

Test it — Open with an odd object in a room. Don't explain what it has to do with anything until several paragraphs later.

Ocean Vuong — "The Weight of Our Living"

Tense Present tense, even for past events Narrator 1st person, urgent, sensory

Open in medias res with fragments; use section breaks (asterisks) to shift pace and register.

  1. Open with two very short declarative sentences that raise a mystery ("There should be tears. There should be a reason.")
  2. Drop straight into a scene already in progress, using present tense and sentence fragments to create speed.
  3. Load the scene with slow-motion sensory detail even as the pace feels fast.
  4. Mark a section break with an asterisk, then shift tone — from crisis to reflection, from present crisis to a calmer present.
  5. Let each new section reframe the previous one instead of explaining it.

Test it — Write a present-tense crisis opening in fragments, mark a break, then write a slower, reflective section that recontextualizes it.

Brian Doyle — "Leap"

Tense Past tense reportage, shifting to 1st person Narrator Journalistic witness, until the "I" arrives late

Accumulate other people's witness testimony before the essay's own voice speaks.

  1. Report what several named observers saw or said, one after another, in flat journalistic style.
  2. Repeat one central image (hands, held hands) across multiple accounts so it accumulates weight through repetition.
  3. Bring in outside texts (scripture, quotation) to widen the frame beyond the personal.
  4. Let the writer's own "I" enter only after the accumulation is complete, and keep it brief.
  5. End by returning to the central image one last time, now carrying the full weight of everything before it.

Test it — Report what three different people saw or said about one event, then let your own single-sentence reaction close it.

Maya Angelou — "I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings"

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person, child's perspective narrated by an adult writer

Repeat a fragment of remembered speech as a refrain that structures the whole scene.

  1. Open with the refrain line itself, isolated, before any context is given.
  2. Return to the refrain two or three more times, each time with more of the surrounding scene revealed.
  3. Let interior fantasy (the child's escape narrative) interrupt and expand between refrains.
  4. Build physical humiliation in concrete sensory detail without naming the emotion directly.
  5. Close the scene, then step back into an adult reflective register that reframes the whole memory.

Test it — Pick one line someone said to you. Let it interrupt your paragraph three times, each time with more context revealed.

Lindsay Starck — "Baikal (fiction)"

Tense Present tense Narrator 3rd person, alternating between two characters' interiority

Structure each section as a question, answered fast, then expanded.

  1. Open each section with a direct question in capital letters.
  2. Answer the question in one quick present-tense sentence immediately after.
  3. Expand the answer for a paragraph, moving between memory and present action.
  4. Alternate whose interiority the question addresses, so two characters' inner lives interleave without a scene break.
  5. Let unanswered questions carry unresolved tension forward into the next section.

Test it — Write a scene in Q&A chunks: pose a question, answer it in one line, then let the answer unspool for a paragraph.

C.D. Wright — "One With Others (exercise)"

Tense Varies by paragraph — deliberately Narrator Documentary collage narrator

Shift diction mid-piece from legalistic disclaimer to lyric imagery.

  1. Open in flat, formal disclaimer language, as if writing legal boilerplate about your own material.
  2. Let that formal language start to lyricize partway through the same paragraph.
  3. Shift the camera to the wider world — place, weather, historical detail — using regional diction specific to that time and place.
  4. Let a very intimate, sensory fragment (a death, a dialogue) interrupt the documentary tone without warning.
  5. Keep each of these tonal registers short — this technique depends on brevity, not explanation.

Test it — Write a formal disclaimer paragraph about your own material, then let it break into lyric language partway through.

Phillip Lopate — "on structure and the 'boredom meter'" (Asymptote interview)

Source Craft interview, not a single text Applies to revision and structural pacing

Let structure emerge from impatience with your own smoothness — when a passage goes safe and settled, push it toward the harder, deeper version underneath instead of following the outline.

  1. Draft the passage the "expected" way first — you need something to push against.
  2. Read it back and locate the moment it starts to feel too easy or too pleased with itself.
  3. Ask directly: what's the harder, less flattering thing underneath this passage?
  4. Rewrite from that harder answer, even if it complicates what you just wrote.
  5. Let the "boredom" itself function as your outline — follow it instead of a pre-set structure.

Test it — Take a paragraph that feels too settled and rewrite it by asking, "what's the harder thing underneath this?"

Phillip Lopate — "on ending an essay" (Asymptote interview)

Source Craft interview, not a single text Applies to closing moves, resisting resolution

Resist the sermon — essays don't need a pyramidal, resolved ending like a short story; let them stop on a small, specific burst of realization rather than a summarizing moral.

  1. Notice when you're reaching for a general lesson to "wrap up" the piece.
  2. Cut the generalization and look for the smallest, most specific image still available to you.
  3. End on that concrete detail instead — let it carry the weight without explaining it.
  4. Resist adding one more sentence that tells the reader what it means.
  5. Trust that an unresolved, modest ending can be more honest than a tidy one.

Test it — Write an ending that stops at one small concrete observation instead of a general lesson.

§02

Lists & Repetition as Structure

Ayşe Papatya Bucak — "An Address to My Fellow Faculty..."

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person, intimate despite a formal occasion

Repeat an anaphoric phrase, alternating what follows, until a final scene delivers the turn.

  1. Choose a repeatable frame phrase tied to your role ("My work is...", "My job is...").
  2. Alternate what follows it — sometimes a verb ("to write"), sometimes a noun ("a novel") — to keep the rhythm from flattening.
  3. Vary paragraph length: some items get one line, others get several, so the list breathes instead of marching.
  4. Let concrete, small physical details (an ant drowned in a drop of water) sit next to abstract admissions (fear, grief).
  5. End the list by breaking form entirely: one small real scene that gives the whole repetition its emotional payoff.

Test it — Repeat "My [role] is..." ten times, alternating verb-led and noun-led completions, then end on one small concrete scene.

Natalie Diaz — "Top Ten Reasons Why Indians Are Good at Basketball"

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person plural, authoritative and funny

Use a numbered list with a confident comic persona to carry serious material without pausing to explain it.

  1. Set a playful frame in item #1 that signals humor is allowed even though the subject is serious.
  2. Let items jump associatively — church to Walt Whitman to weapons — without transitions between them.
  3. Keep the tone matter-of-fact and tongue-in-cheek even when the content underneath is charged.
  4. Use one item to pivot into direct historical or political material, still inside the joke structure.
  5. End on the longest, most lyrical item — let the list format finally break open into full sentences and imagery.

Test it — Write a numbered "top ten" list on a loaded topic. Stay funny and confident even as the material turns serious.

Margaret Renkl — "Things I Knew / Didn't Know When I Was Six"

Tense Present tense Narrator Child's-eye facts, adult's hindsight underneath

Write two parallel lists — a child's plain facts, then the adult truths hidden beneath them — in the same flat tone.

  1. Write list one as plain, authoritative statements a child would actually believe as fact.
  2. Address some items to "you" and some as flat declarations, mixing registers within the same list.
  3. Write list two using the same flat tone, but let the content reveal what the child couldn't yet understand.
  4. Never comment on the gap between the two lists — let the reader do that work.
  5. Order both lists so the final item lands hardest, without being the most dramatic.

Test it — Write a "things I knew" list about an age or moment, then a second "things I didn't know" list in the same flat tone.

Ira Sukrungruang — "Because, The Ferguson Verdict"

Tense Past and present, braided Narrator 1st person, accumulating

Chain cause-and-effect fragments, each beginning "Because," braiding personal history with a larger pattern.

  1. Start with a specific personal fact, prefaced by "Because."
  2. Let the next "Because" sentence widen slightly — from personal detail to a related cultural or historical fact.
  3. Keep alternating scale: personal, wider, personal, wider — never staying in one register too long.
  4. Let some "Because" sentences get very short ("Because I was eight.") to vary rhythm against longer ones.
  5. Break the chain near the end to name the pattern itself ("Because the word 'because' demands cause and effect") before returning to more "Because" sentences.

Test it — Write ten sentences in a row that all start with "Because," moving between a personal memory and a wider pattern it belongs to.

Matthew Dickman — "Trouble (poem)"

Tense Present and past, mixed within lines Narrator 1st person, catalog-driven

Catalog real deaths in a flat, almost gossipy tone, then pivot the ending to small ordinary self-care.

  1. List a real death or loss (a public figure, a stranger) in one flat sentence.
  2. Interrupt the catalog with an unrelated, mundane observation ("I like the little soaps in hotel bathrooms") — no transition.
  3. Continue alternating catalog entries and small mundane asides without signaling the pattern.
  4. Let the catalog escalate toward the personal loss that is the poem's real subject, still in the same flat tone.
  5. End on two or three small, ordinary acts of self-care, delivered with no more weight than the deaths were.

Test it — List several losses or hard facts in one breath, then end on two small, ordinary things you do to keep going.

Jack Kerouac — "America in World History"

Tense Present tense argument Narrator 1st person, declarative and certain

Build an enumerated argument where each numbered point stacks evidence toward one big claim.

  1. State the big claim up front, with full confidence, before any evidence is given.
  2. Number each supporting point so the argument reads as building, not meandering.
  3. Let each point mix personal feeling ("I feel young and unfulfilled") with a historical or cultural claim.
  4. Use repetition of key phrases across points to keep the argument's throughline audible.
  5. Don't hedge — let the confident, declarative register carry through even where the claims are debatable.

Test it — State a big personal claim, then back it with three numbered points that mix feeling and fact.

§03

Persona-Narrator & Point of View

James Baldwin — "A Letter to My Nephew"

Tense Present tense address, past-tense memory Narrator 1st person, epistolary, addressed to one named person

Address one real, named person directly for the entire piece.

  1. Open by naming the recipient directly ("Dear James") and stating the difficulty of writing this at all.
  2. Move between address to the nephew and address to the nephew's father — the letter carries two relationships at once.
  3. State the accusation or hard truth plainly, without softening it for the reader.
  4. Return to direct address ("my dear namesake") to re-ground the piece in intimacy after the hardest passages.
  5. Close on instruction — tell the addressee what to do with what you've told them.

Test it — Write a letter to one real person, naming them throughout, saying the hard thing you'd say only to them.

Kwame Dawes — "Death (poem)"

Tense Present tense, addressed to "you" Narrator 2nd person, implicating the reader

Address the reader as "you" throughout, folding them into experiences they didn't choose.

  1. Open with a small, plausible "you" action (your dog dies) that any reader could imagine doing.
  2. Escalate the "you" actions in small, deliberate steps, each one slightly worse than the last.
  3. Keep the tone matter-of-fact even as the actions become disturbing — no editorializing.
  4. Let the escalation build toward a larger claim about power, delivered still in "you" address.
  5. End without releasing the reader from the "you" — no return to "I" to soften the implication.

Test it — Rewrite a personal memory addressed to "you" instead of "I," making the reader complicit in the action.

Toni Cade Bambara — "A Sort of Preface"

Tense Present and future hypothetical Narrator 1st person addressing "you" mid-piece

Use breathless run-on sentences addressed to "you" that implicate the reader in a scene the narrator denies is autobiographical.

  1. Open with a flat claim (writing autobiographical fiction "does no good") that the rest of the piece will undercut.
  2. Build one very long run-on sentence describing the imagined consequences, piling clause on clause without a full stop.
  3. Shift into "you" mid-sentence, making the reader the one who gets caught, not the narrator.
  4. Add a second scenario (a friend's reaction) that mirrors the first, extending the run-on technique.
  5. Close with one short, dry sentence that undercuts everything — comic deflection as the final craft move.

Test it — Write one very long run-on sentence addressed to "you" about a fear you have of being found out.

Nick Flynn — "Ulysses / Another Bullshit Night in Suck City"

Tense Present tense generalizations, then past tense specifics Narrator "1st-as-3rd" — general "some"/"many" until the very end

Write in a general register ("Some sons...", "Many fathers...") for most of the piece, shifting to "I" only at the close.

  1. Open with general statements about a category of people ("Many fathers are gone") using "some" and "many," no names.
  2. Use anaphora — repeating the same opening word or structure — to build rhythm across these general statements.
  3. Include one small imagined mini-scene, still in the general register, to make the abstraction concrete.
  4. Introduce a turn phrase ("Some sons need to...") that starts narrowing the focus toward the real subject.
  5. Shift to "I" and past tense only in the final lines — this is where the piece's real weight lands.

Test it — Write about a personal experience using "some people" and "many" instead of "I," switching to "I" only in the last two sentences.

Joan Wickersham — "The Suicide Index"

Tense Present tense Narrator 2nd person, used for emotional distance

Use "you" to create distance from a numbing experience, narrated in flat present-tense routine.

  1. Narrate a numb daily routine in second person, one small task at a time (drop off, sit, wait).
  2. Insert a remembered contrasting moment (laughter, joy) using the same flat "you" address.
  3. Let the remembered moment include real, specific dialogue to keep it grounded, not abstract.
  4. Return to the present-tense numbness, now shadowed by the memory just described.
  5. End on a small physical detail (a child leaning against you) rather than a stated emotion.

Test it — Narrate a numb, routine day in second person, staying flat and factual even as the material underneath is not.

Julie Otsuka — "Buddha in the Attic (fiction)"

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person plural ("we") speaking for a whole group

Use a collective "we" narrator to hold many individual, sometimes contradictory, experiences at once.

  1. Open with a collective claim about the group ("On the boat we were mostly virgins") that sets the shared premise.
  2. Follow with a fast list of variations within the group — some had this, some had that — without naming individuals.
  3. Repeat a structural anchor phrase ("On the boat...") to organize new movements within the collective voice.
  4. Let individual, specific, even contradictory details sit inside the "we" without resolving into one single story.
  5. Trust the accumulation of variation to create a fuller portrait than any single "I" could give.

Test it — Write a scene using only "we," letting the collective voice hold many different individual details inside it.

Zadie Smith — "A Woman with a Little Dog"

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person, mostly outward-facing

Build a character almost entirely through outward observation, revealing the narrator only in glancing asides.

  1. Open with a wry, affectionate claim about the other person ("The funny thing about Barbara...").
  2. Describe physical details and habits in a steady accumulation, without pausing to reflect on them.
  3. Let the narrator's own life surface only in brief comparative asides ("she smokes the way I used to").
  4. Include one piece of direct quoted dialogue that reveals more about the subject than description could.
  5. End on an unexpected shift in the subject's tone (quiet instead of loud) that recontextualizes everything before it.

Test it — Describe a neighbor or acquaintance in detail for a full page, revealing yourself only in small aside phrases.

Jerald Walker — "How to Make a Slave"

Tense Present tense imperative Narrator 2nd person, instructing your own past self

Use second-person imperative to narrate your own past as a set of instructions.

  1. Open with a concrete imperative action ("Gather scissors, construction paper...") that sets a scene without explanation.
  2. Continue in imperative verbs ("Take," "Realize," "Smile") to narrate what happened as if instructing the reader to do it.
  3. Let time jump forward using the same imperative structure ("Find a funny part" years later).
  4. Insert real dialogue inside the instructions without breaking the second-person frame.
  5. Let the imperative distance make hard material easier to say directly than "I" would allow.

Test it — Narrate a memory as a set of second-person imperative instructions: "Gather... Take... Realize..."

Jerald Walker — "Dragon Slayers"

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person, confined to one scene

Confine the whole essay to one scene of dialogue, mixing quoted and summarized speech to control pacing.

  1. Open with a first line that states the essay's whole tension in one sentence.
  2. Follow immediately with the idea the other character represents, stated plainly.
  3. Alternate quoted dialogue with summarized dialogue — use quotes for the sharpest lines, summary for connective tissue.
  4. Let the narrator's internal reactions surface as brief asides between exchanges, not as separate reflective passages.
  5. End the scene at its natural social breaking point, without following either character further.

Test it — Write one full scene of dialogue only, mixing exact quotes with summarized lines, and let the idea emerge from the exchange itself.

Joseph Millar — "Sole Custody (poem)"

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person, single sustained domestic scene

Stay inside one ordinary evening scene, letting small details carry the emotional weight.

  1. Open with a specific, unglamorous action (a bike ride, a purchase) that grounds the scene immediately.
  2. Add physical, sensory detail about the other person (clothing, food) without commentary.
  3. Let one line of the other person's dialogue arrive unexpectedly, shifting the tone.
  4. Register worry or love only through a small physical observation (dirt under a fingernail), never stated directly.
  5. End on a metaphor that reframes the whole domestic scene at a larger scale (sailors, an ocean).

Test it — Write one ordinary domestic scene in detail, and let one line of the other person's dialogue shift the tone without comment.

Dana Levin — "Lenin's Bath (poem)"

Tense Present tense Narrator 3rd person, observing a historical figure

Use a persona poem to inhabit a real historical scene from a close third-person vantage.

  1. Open with a physical action (lifting a body) described with unexpected tenderness.
  2. Let a piece of dark humor or irreverence interrupt the tenderness without warning.
  3. Introduce a second character (an observer) whose interior thoughts the poem can access.
  4. Use precise, clinical/scientific vocabulary (bacteria, embalming fluid) to keep the tone controlled.
  5. End on a small image of vulnerability (hungry flies, cold windows) that undercuts the historical grandeur.

Test it — Write a scene inhabiting a real historical or public figure at a private, unglamorous moment, mixing tenderness and irreverence.

Phillip Lopate — "The Tender Stranger" (nonfiction exercise)

Tense Adult retrospective, matter-of-fact Narrator 1st person, older narrating a childhood self

Ground the persona-narrator in one specific, dated childhood scene, then let a calm, matter-of-fact adult tone contain the child's unspoken longing.

  1. Choose one specific, dated childhood incident rather than a general period of your life.
  2. Narrate it in a calm, matter-of-fact adult voice — resist dramatizing what the child felt.
  3. Let physical detail and action carry the scene without naming the emotion underneath.
  4. Allow the unspoken feeling to build until it's nearly overflowing.
  5. Name the feeling exactly once, in one plain sentence, at the moment it can no longer stay unspoken.

Test it — Write a childhood incident as one scene in a matter-of-fact adult voice, and allow yourself exactly one sentence naming the feeling underneath.

§04

Sentence Craft & Tonal Control

Jhumpa Lahiri — "In the Sun"

Tense Present tense Narrator 1st person, observant, confident

Use the word "but" repeatedly as a structural hinge, creating a collage of simultaneous experience.

  1. Open with one sentence that names a tension (protests and helicopters) before pivoting on "but."
  2. Let the "but" introduce a competing mood (sunlight, pleasure) that doesn't cancel the first, just sits beside it.
  3. Continue the piece with several more "but" pivots, each introducing a small new observation.
  4. Include brief quoted dialogue from strangers to widen the collage beyond the narrator's own view.
  5. End on an abstract word (forsaken) that the concrete details have earned, without explaining it.

Test it — Write a paragraph where every few sentences pivot on "but," stacking contradictory observations into one mood.

Claire Messud — "The Road to Damascus"

Tense Past tense, memory within memory Narrator 1st person, generational

Build long, cumulative, clause-stacked sentences that carry a lifetime of memory in one breath.

  1. Start the sentence with a small concrete image (a chicken on a balcony).
  2. Add clause after clause, each one a new memory-fragment, connected by commas and semicolons rather than periods.
  3. Let the sentence range across years and locations without ever fully stopping.
  4. Bring in a specific, unexpected detail (a monk's messy apartment) to keep the long sentence grounded, not abstract.
  5. Land the sentence on the emotional core it was building toward the whole time.

Test it — Write one sentence, at least eight clauses long, that carries a whole memory from start to finish without a period.

Lucia Perillo — "A Glimpse"

Tense Present tense frame, past tense narrative Narrator 1st person, resistant to the expected script

Open with a clinical anecdote, then explicitly resist the "brave" response the reader expects.

  1. Open with a factual, almost clinical anecdote about an animal or biological process.
  2. Draw an explicit parallel between that anecdote and the narrator's own body or diagnosis.
  3. Narrate the diagnosis scene in flat, procedural language, resisting melodrama.
  4. State plainly the response you're "supposed" to have, then contradict it directly.
  5. Bring in an outside reference (a story, a myth) that reframes the resistance as a shared human pattern, not just personal defiance.

Test it — Open with a clinical or biological fact, then write the sentence where you refuse the response you're "supposed" to have.

Eudora Welty — "One Writer's Beginnings"

Tense Past tense Narrator 1st person, retrospective memoirist

Root memoir in small sensory childhood objects, each one quietly foreshadowing who the writer became.

  1. Choose one household object (a clock, a telescope) and describe it with precise sensory detail.
  2. Let a small family scene form around the object — who used it, what they said.
  3. Include a quoted line of family dialogue that reveals a family value without stating it as a theme.
  4. End the object's paragraph with a quiet, understated connection to the writer's later craft or sensibility.
  5. Repeat this pattern with a different object, letting the accumulated objects build a portrait indirectly.

Test it — Pick three childhood objects and write one paragraph each, ending each with a hint of who they were shaping you to be.

Natasha Tretheway — "Memorial Drive (exercise)"

Tense Present-tense description of a past photograph Narrator 1st person, hindsight-driven

Study a photograph in patient detail, letting hindsight reframe what it "really" shows as the paragraph goes on.

  1. Describe the photograph's surface details first — clothing, posture, setting — with no interpretation.
  2. Introduce competing stories you've told yourself about the photograph's meaning.
  3. State plainly which story you prefer to believe, and why, even while admitting uncertainty.
  4. Return to the image's visual details, now reading new significance into them (the light, the shadow).
  5. End on what the photographer may have unintentionally captured — meaning arriving from outside anyone's control.

Test it — Describe a real photograph in detail, then let your last two sentences reveal what you now know that the photo couldn't show.

Peter Ho Davies — "A Lie Someone Told You About Yourself (fiction)"

Tense Past tense Narrator Close 3rd person

Use short floating paragraphs, separated by white space, to mimic fragmented thought.

  1. Write one thought as a very short, self-contained paragraph — often just one or two sentences.
  2. Leave a blank line before the next paragraph instead of connecting them with transitions.
  3. Let each fragment repeat and slightly revise the previous one ("There was a chance... There was a chance...").
  4. Include one piece of dialogue that interrupts the internal fragments with an external voice.
  5. Let the accumulation of fragments, not any single sentence, carry the emotional payload.

Test it — Write a close-third scene in short floating paragraphs, each one a single thought, separated by blank lines.

Kevin Wilson — "Biology (fiction)"

Tense Present tense frame, past tense memory Narrator 1st person

Let grief for a minor figure unlock a larger, unspoken loneliness.

  1. Open with ordinary, almost bureaucratic news (a Facebook post) about someone minor in the narrator's life.
  2. Register an unexpected emotional reaction (crying without noticing) before explaining who the person was.
  3. Flash back to concrete, specific childhood detail that shows why this person mattered, without stating it.
  4. Let a second character in the present (the boyfriend) notice the reaction and ask about it, giving the narrator no way to explain.
  5. End on the real, larger loss the minor figure stood in for — implied, never named directly.

Test it — Write about grief for someone minor in your life, and let the real, larger loss surface only by implication.

Phillip Lopate — "on 'thinking against yourself'" (Asymptote interview)

Source Craft interview, not a single text Applies to stance, self-complication

After drafting a claim, argue back against it on the page — essayistic authority comes from catching and complicating your own conviction, not defending it.

  1. Write a paragraph asserting something you actually believe, stated with full confidence.
  2. Reread it and locate the exception, or the version of you that disagrees.
  3. Write a second paragraph arguing against the first, as seriously as you argued for it.
  4. Don't rush to resolve the contradiction — let both paragraphs stand.
  5. If a synthesis arrives, let it be smaller and more qualified than either original claim.

Test it — Write a paragraph asserting something you believe, then write a second paragraph that disagrees with your first.
